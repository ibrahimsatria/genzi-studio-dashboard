Phase 1 — Build proof without waiting for paying clients

You're trying to sell from zero proof. The fastest fix is to manufacture proof across three parallel tracks — spec work, discounted real work, and live documentation. You don't need a client to start any of them.

1.1Spec work — 3 local business rebrands
▼

Pick 3 businesses in Brunei with visibly poor branding — a kopitiam, a retail shop, a service business like a tutoring centre or salon. Design their complete brand identity as if you were hired: logo system, colour palette, type pairing, application mockups, social templates. No permission needed. You own all rights. You get full creative control.

Why it works: You get portfolio pieces with zero dependency on anyone else's timeline or budget. And once the piece is done, you can DM the real business — turning passive portfolio work into active prospecting simultaneously.

Best targets in BSB: F&B spots with no visual coherence, night market vendors that have grown beyond a stall, service businesses with DIY-looking identity, new businesses that just launched with no brand at all.

1.21–2 real clients at introductory rate
▼

Take on 1–2 real businesses at 50–70% off your future rate, framed as an "introductory partnership" — not charity. The discount is specific to this first project only. In exchange: you control the creative direction, you get full publishing rights in writing, and you get a written testimonial on delivery day.

Why charge something: Even BND 150–300 completely changes the dynamic. Clients who pay show up to feedback calls, respond to messages, make decisions on time, and respect your process. Free clients ghost, delay, and become demanding because they feel they have nothing at stake.

Hard rules: Cap at 2 clients at this rate. State clearly upfront that the next project is at your standard rate. Clients who push back on that are exactly who you want to filter out at this stage.

1.3Document everything publicly in real time
▼

Your personal Instagram (ibrahimsatria._) is where this happens. Post the process — not just the polished final output. Brief → concept exploration → revision → result. Show what got rejected and why. This builds authority faster than a finished portfolio because it's live proof you know how to think through a problem.

Goal by end of Phase 1: 3–5 portfolio pieces, 2–3 real testimonials, a case study format you can repeat for every future project automatically.

Phase 2 — Activate your channels (Month 1–3)

Once you have 3–5 pieces and 2–3 testimonials, you have something to point at. Now you run two tracks simultaneously — digital presence and direct outreach. Both feed each other.

2.1Two-account Instagram strategy
▼

@genzistudio posts results: before/afters, case studies, transformation stories, and the agency's strategic POV on branding. This is credibility content — for people already looking for a creative agency.

@ibrahimsatria._ posts perspective: why local SME branding fails, what growth looks like in Brunei, founder journey, sharp opinions. This is trust content — for people who buy into you before they know the agency exists.

Always link the two accounts clearly. The personal account is the top of the funnel. The agency account is where trust converts to enquiry. This is why both need consistent activity, and your bio on both should point to the other.

2.2Direct outreach — lead with observation, not pitch
▼

Target F&B, retail, and professional services (legal, medical, accounting) — they have real budgets and visible branding problems. Lead with a specific observation about their business, not a generic service pitch:

"I noticed your Instagram hasn't been updated in 3 months — I had a few specific ideas for your business. Would you be open to a 20-minute conversation?"

This frames you as someone who did their homework, not someone mass-DMing with a template. In Brunei's small, relationship-driven market, that distinction matters enormously.

2.3LinkedIn + local networking
▼

LinkedIn is underused in Brunei — which is your advantage. Post the same strategic perspective content you'd put on your personal Instagram, but in a longer-form B2B register. Business owners in BSB are increasingly active here. One good post per week is enough to become the most visible young creative strategist in the local feed.

For local networking: the Brunei Chinese Chamber of Commerce, BEDB events, youth entrepreneurship groups. In a market this small, one warm introduction from the right person can open five doors. You don't need advertising. You need to be consistently visible at the right places.

Goal by end of Phase 2: 2–3 genuine discovery conversations per week, 1 paid client per month minimum.

Phase 3 — Scale and convert (Month 3+)
3.1Package your services into clear offers
▼

Stop selling time. Start selling outcomes. Three tiers work well for Brunei SMEs:

Brand Starter: Logo + brand guide + social templates. One-time project. Entry point for smaller businesses or those skeptical about investing in brand.

Brand Growth: Full identity + 3 months of content strategy and social management. Mid-tier. The sweet spot for F&B and retail.

Brand Scale: Complete identity + ongoing retainer — strategy, content, reporting, optimisation. Highest value, highest relationship. This is where your real revenue comes from.

Clear packages remove the "so what does this actually cost?" anxiety that kills deals in the discovery stage. Clients in Brunei respond well to structured offers because it feels safe and professional.

3.2Raise rates as proof accumulates
▼

Every completed project is a rate justification. After your first 3 paid clients, review your pricing. After your first 5, raise it. The competition problem solves itself once you have proof — your editorial luxury aesthetic is already genuinely differentiated from what most local agencies produce in BSB. That's a real moat. You just need the work to prove it.

Goal by end of Phase 3: Predictable pipeline, 3–5 retainer clients, a referral system that brings new leads passively without you actively hunting.

Build your Portfolio

The strategy to go from zero proof to 3–5 compelling portfolio pieces — without waiting for a paying client to hire you first.

✦ Read this section to earn +50 XP

A portfolio piece is not a beautiful design. It is a story with a problem, a strategy, and a result. The design is the evidence.

— The standard that separates professionals from practitioners
The 3 sources of portfolio work
Source 1
Spec work
Design for a real business without being hired. You pick the client, write the brief, control all creative decisions, own all rights. Zero dependency on anyone's schedule or budget.
Best for: speed and creative control
Source 2
Discounted real work
1–2 real clients at a reduced rate in exchange for full publishing rights and a written testimonial. Gets you real case studies with real relationships behind them.
Best for: testimonials and credibility
Source 3
Personal projects
Design something that matters to you — your own agency brand, a concept brand for a sector you want to serve. Shows passion, range, and where your aesthetic instincts naturally go.
Best for: showing your aesthetic range
What separates strong work from weak work
✗Weak: gallery of final outputs
▼

A logo on a white background. Three mockups on phone screens. Maybe a colour palette. This is what 90% of new designers put in their portfolio. It tells a prospective client nothing about how you think, what problem you were solving, whether you can handle a real engagement, or whether the design decision was yours or the client's.

The question it fails to answer: "Can I trust this person to understand my business and make the right decisions?" Without strategic context, the answer is always uncertain.

✓Strong: narrative case study with problem and result
▼

A case study follows this structure: 1. The business and its problem — who they are, who they serve, what their current branding fails to communicate. 2. Your strategic direction — what you decided to do and why. What you rejected and why. 3. The work — the complete visual system. 4. The outcome — what changed or what would change if this were implemented in full.

For spec work, the "outcome" section can be forward-looking: "A coherent brand identity that would allow [Business] to charge a premium and attract a more discerning customer." That's honest and still compelling to a prospective client in the same sector.

The before/after contrast is your most powerful single tool. A screenshot of their current Instagram next to your redesigned concept — the gap does the selling for you before you've said a word.

Pricing Strategy

How to approach free and discounted work so it builds your reputation rather than devaluing it. The framing you use makes all the difference.

✦ Read this section to earn +50 XP

You're not doing free work. You're buying an asset — a case study — at a negotiated price. That reframe changes every conversation you have about it.

— The mindset shift that protects your positioning
The 3-tier deal structure
Tier 1 — Spec
No client needed
BND 0 your time only
  • You choose the business
  • No client approval needed
  • Full creative control, all rights
  • No delivery pressure or delays
No testimonial — but you can DM afterwards
Tier 2 — Recommended
Introductory partnership
50–70% off future rate
  • Real client relationship
  • Written testimonial at delivery
  • Full publishing rights in writing
  • Money exchanged = mutual respect
Cap strictly at 2 clients at this rate
Tier 3 — Value swap
Work for non-cash value
BND 0 trade for value
  • Trade design for their service
  • Builds local network naturally
  • Feels mutual, not one-sided
  • Formalize the exchange in writing
Only if the trade genuinely benefits you
Why Tier 2 beats free every time
→The psychology of charging something — even a small amount
▼

When a client pays even BND 150–300 for what would normally cost BND 1,000+, they show up to feedback calls. They respond to messages within a day. They make creative decisions instead of postponing. They respect the timeline. Paying clients treat you like a professional because they've placed real value on the engagement.

Free clients often ghost, delay, become demanding, or pivot the project indefinitely — because they feel they have nothing to lose. The cost to them of wasting your time is zero.

Charging something also changes how you show up. You take the brief more seriously. You set a scope. You create a delivery expectation. The whole dynamic becomes professional on both sides, which ultimately means better work and a better case study.

→How to frame the introductory rate conversation
▼

"I'm selectively taking on a small number of introductory partnerships this quarter where I offer a significantly reduced rate in exchange for the ability to use the work in my portfolio and a testimonial at the end. I think your business would make a compelling case study — would that arrangement interest you?"

Notice the structure: "selectively" signals demand and exclusivity. "introductory partnership" is mutual and professional rather than charitable. "compelling case study" flatters them while explaining your honest motivation. You're choosing them, not begging them.

Non-negotiable conditions on every deal
1Full publishing rights — always confirm in writing first
▼

Before starting any project at any rate, confirm in writing that you can post the work on your website, Instagram, portfolio platforms, and pitches — forever, without needing permission each time. A message thread confirmation counts. A signed brief is better. Without this, you might do your best work ever and be unable to show it because the client got anxious about public visibility later.

2Written testimonial — ask on delivery day at peak happiness
▼

Ask for the testimonial the moment you deliver the final work — when the excitement is highest and they're seeing the full transformation for the first time. Push gently for specificity: "How do you think this will change how customers perceive you?" A quote about results — "this makes us look like we've been doing this for 10 years" — is ten times more persuasive than "Ibrahim was great to work with and very professional."

3Hard expiry on the discount — say it clearly before starting
▼

"Just so we're aligned — this rate is specific to this first project. If we work together again, or if you refer someone to me, it would be at my standard rate. Happy to share that now so there's no surprise later."

Most good clients respect this completely. The ones who push back are exactly who you want to filter out early. This also subtly signals that you expect the work to be good enough to justify working together again — which creates a quiet confidence that's hard to replicate any other way.

4You lead creative direction — cap revisions at two rounds
▼

Cap revisions at two rounds from the beginning: "I'll present three strong directions at the start, then we refine together — I find two rounds of revisions is usually plenty to get to something we're both genuinely proud of." This is a portfolio piece. If the client overrides every decision, the final work won't reflect your ability or aesthetic — which defeats the entire purpose of the arrangement. Two rounds also creates natural urgency and prevents projects from becoming endless.

Pre-project checklist
  • Publishing rights confirmed in writing before any work begins+15 XP
  • Rate and full scope defined clearly and agreed before starting+15 XP
  • Hard expiry on the rate communicated upfront in the conversation+15 XP
  • Revision rounds capped at 2 and stated at project kickoff+15 XP
  • Testimonial request planned and ready to send on delivery day+15 XP
Execution

Spec Work Process

The complete 5-stage system for creating portfolio pieces without a client — and how to turn each finished piece into a real door opener.

✦ Read this section to earn +50 XP

Spec work done badly looks like fan art. Done well, it looks like a real case study that happens to not have a paying client behind it. The brief you write before starting is what makes the difference.

— The standard to aim for
The 5 stages in full
01Pick the right target business
▼

Don't just pick businesses with bad logos. Pick businesses whose problem a future paying client could recognise themselves in. The best candidates have an existing product or service worth being proud of — you're showing what they could look like if they invested properly in their brand.

The right target has three qualities:

1. A visible branding problem — inconsistent social feed, an amateurish logo, no visual system at all, mismatched fonts and colours across touchpoints.

2. A product or service people actually want — a failing business makes the "what could be" story hard to tell convincingly. A good business with bad branding makes it effortless.

3. Relevance to your target clients — if you want to serve F&B businesses, do F&B spec work. The portfolio should attract the exact type of client you want to keep working with.

Avoid: Businesses that are objectively failing or closing — the upgrade story falls flat. Also avoid anything so obscure that no prospective client could place it.

02Write a real brief — before opening Figma or Illustrator
▼

This is the step most people skip, and it's what separates a portfolio piece from a collection of pretty things. Before any design software, write down:

Business overview: What do they do, who do they serve, what makes them genuinely good at what they do?

The branding problem: What does their current visual identity fail to communicate? What first impression does it create, versus the one it should?

Target audience: Who is the ideal customer for this business? What visual language do they respond to? What signals trust vs. distrust to them?

Strategic direction: What feeling should the new identity create? What values need to come through? What aesthetic territory are you working in — and what references are you drawing from?

Constraints: Industry conventions, cultural context specific to Brunei, the appropriate budget register (premium vs. approachable vs. playful).

This brief becomes part of your case study and is often what most impresses prospective clients — because it proves you think strategically before touching any design tool.

03Do the full scope — not just a logo
▼

A logo alone is not a portfolio piece. Clients buy confidence in a complete system. Show them you can deliver it all the way:

Minimum: Logo + variations · Colour palette · Type pairing · Business card mockup · Instagram profile or feed mockup · One social content template

Strong additions: Signage or packaging mockup · Brand guideline one-pager · Before/after social feed comparison · Short video showing the system in use

The question a partial portfolio piece leaves in a client's mind is "can they actually finish this?" A complete system answers it definitively before they've asked.

04Present as a case study — not a gallery
▼

The format that converts prospective clients consistently:

Opening paragraph: The business, who they serve, and what their branding problem was. Include the before screenshot. Be specific and respectful — you're diagnosing an opportunity, not mocking them.

Strategy paragraph: What direction you chose and why. What you rejected and why. What the new identity needs to communicate. This is where your thinking is visible to a prospective client reading the case study.

The work: Full system presented cleanly. Multiple mockups in real-world context. Don't just show the logo floating on white — show it on a business card, on a shopfront, in a social post as it would actually appear.

Closing line: What would change. Keep it grounded and forward-looking: "A coherent identity that positions [Business] as the premium choice in their category, allowing them to charge accordingly."

05DM the actual business once the piece is done
▼

This is where spec work becomes doubly valuable. Once the piece is done, message the actual business owner. You'll get one of three outcomes — all of them positive:

They love it and want to use it → potential paying client. They're flattered and remember you → warm relationship for when they're ready. They don't respond → no loss, you still have the portfolio piece to show everyone else.

"Hi [Name] — I work in brand design and recently put together a concept for [Business Name] as part of some personal portfolio work. I'd love to share it with you if you're open to seeing it. No strings attached at all — I just think there's a great brand story in what you've built and wanted to show you what it could look like."

Why this lands in Brunei specifically: The market is small enough that a genuine, personal message about a specific business stands out immediately. Most owners are used to generic pitches. A message that's clearly about their business — with real creative work already done behind it — is almost impossible not to respond to.

First spec project — your action checklist
  • Identify your first spec target and confirm it meets the 3 criteria+20 XP
  • Write the full brief (audience, problem, direction, constraints)+20 XP
  • Complete the full scope — logo system, palette, type, 3+ mockups+20 XP
  • Write the one-page case study following problem → strategy → result+20 XP
  • DM the real business and share the work with no strings attached+20 XP

The conversion funnel between your accounts
→How a stranger becomes a paying client
▼

Step 1 — Discovery: They see your personal content shared by someone they trust, or it shows up in their feed because the local creative scene is small and the algorithm notices.

Step 2 — Following: They like one or two posts, scroll your feed, decide your perspective is useful enough to follow. They're not a client yet — they're an audience member.

Step 3 — Cross-over: A post mentions @genzistudio, or a case study lands. They click through. They see proof. They start thinking "their work is good — and I already trust the person behind it."

Step 4 — Trigger: Months later, they need a brand. You're the first name that comes to mind. They DM. You haven't pitched once.

This is what consistent personal content buys you. It's slow at first, then suddenly compounds — because every post adds to the asset, and the local market is small enough that consistency alone creates dominance.

Outreach Playbook

Discovery-first outreach for Brunei SMEs. The scripts, channels, and sequences that start real conversations — not pitches that get ignored.

✦ Read this section to earn +50 XP

Cold pitches don't work in Brunei. Specific observations do. The difference between the two is whether the business owner feels seen — or marketed to.

— The mindset that opens doors in a small, relationship-driven market
The 4 outreach channels — ranked by signal
Channel 1 · Highest signal
Instagram DM after spec work
You did the work first. Message them with the finished concept attached. This is the single highest-converting outreach format you have access to — because it's impossible to read as a generic pitch.
Use for: businesses you've already designed for
Channel 2 · High signal
Warm intro via mutual contact
A friend, former colleague, or someone you've worked with introduces you. The introduction does the trust-building work upfront. In BSB this is the most natural way clients flow — leverage your network deliberately, not passively.
Use for: target businesses one degree away
Channel 3 · Medium signal
Specific-observation DM
Cold DM, but lead with a real observation about their business — not a service pitch. The bar is high: if your observation could apply to any business in the same category, it's not specific enough yet. Rewrite until it couldn't.
Use for: businesses where spec is not yet feasible
Channel 4 · Slow-burn signal
LinkedIn presence + events
Doesn't book clients today. Builds visibility so that when you DM, or when they need a designer, you're already a familiar name. Post weekly. Show up at Chamber of Commerce events, BEDB sessions, youth entrepreneurship meetups.
Use for: long-term positioning
3 outreach scripts you can use this week
01Script — Spec work delivery (Channel 1)
▼

"Hi [Name] — I work in brand design and recently put together a concept for [Business Name] as part of some personal portfolio work. No strings attached — I just thought there was a great brand story in what you've built, and I wanted to show you what it could look like. Happy to share the file if you're open to it."

Why it works: You're not asking for anything. You're offering something. The phrase "no strings attached" disarms the natural defensive reaction owners have to anyone DMing about their business. You've already done the work, so they don't have to imagine — they just have to look.

Follow-up if no reply in 5 days: "Hey — totally understand if it's not the right time. Wanted to send the file over anyway in case it's useful down the line. Take care." Then send it. Send the file regardless. That's the move that surprises them.

02Script — Warm intro request (Channel 2)
▼

"Hey [Mutual Contact] — quick favour. I'm growing my brand design studio (Genzi Studio) and I think [Target Business] could really benefit from a brand refresh. I know you work with / know [Owner Name] — would you be open to a quick introduction? Happy to share my portfolio first so you can decide if it makes sense."

Why it works: You give them an easy out ("if it makes sense"). You share proof upfront so they're not vouching blind. You make the ask small and specific — an intro, not an endorsement. In Brunei, people are willing to make introductions; they just need to feel safe doing it.

What to send them: Your portfolio link, 1–2 lines on why this target business specifically, and a screenshot of one piece of relevant work. Three things, no more. Make it copy-pasteable so they can forward it directly to the owner without rewriting anything.

03Script — Specific-observation cold DM (Channel 3)
▼

"Hi [Name] — I came across [Business] recently and noticed your Instagram hasn't been updated in about 3 months. I had a few specific ideas for how the visual identity could work harder for you. Open to a quick 20-minute conversation? No pitch — just a chat."

Why it works: The specific observation ("3 months") signals you actually looked. The phrase "no pitch — just a chat" disarms expectation of being sold to. The time-boxed ask ("20 minutes") is small enough to say yes to without anxiety.

What to avoid: Anything that could apply to any other business. "I love what you're doing" is dead on arrival. "Your logo could be stronger" is too prescriptive. The sweet spot is specific + neutral + curious — a doctor's observation, not a salesperson's pitch.

The weekly outreach rhythm

Outreach is a numbers game until it becomes a referral game. The first 90 days are about volume in the right direction — specific, considered messages sent consistently to the right sectors. Set a target you can actually hit every week without burning out, and protect that time on your calendar like a client meeting.

Monday
Research & pick targets
Identify 5 specific businesses you'll reach out to this week. Look at their current branding, social activity, customer base. Take notes that will become your specific observation.
Tuesday – Thursday
Send the messages
Send personalised DMs across the 5 targets — spread out, not all at once. Don't bulk-send templated messages. Quality of message beats quantity of outreach in this market every single time.
Friday
Follow up & log results
Follow up on anyone who hasn't replied after 5 days. Log responses in a simple sheet: target, channel, message sent, response, next action. Patterns emerge after 4 weeks that change your approach.
